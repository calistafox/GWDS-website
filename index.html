Coding Worksheets

CARP

CARP stands for Contrast, Alignment, Repetition & Proximity. Four key design priciples which I have focused on to create the designs below. All were created using Abode Indesign.

Contrast

Contrast adds visual interest to a page, drawing the viewer in with visual elements which differ greatly. In my poster, there is a harsh contrast between the backgrounds of the words “true” and “false”, spit with a diagonal line which doubles as the slash in the title, enhancing its impact and capturing the viewer's attention.

Alignment

Alignment refers to every element being visually connected in some way to another element on a page to create a clean, professional feel. My design uses two axes which cross at an angle, I arranged my, with the F from false and film being the central point. I am pleased with this design as it feels sleek and easy to understand while being visually interesting for the eye.

Repetition

Repetition is the principle that visual elements are repeated throughout the design, giving a feeling of unity and comfort to the viewer. For my design, I used the words true/false to create the shape of a clapperboard, to represent the festival being about films.

Proximity

Proximity is the principle of grouping related items together to show them as related and a cohesive group. It minimises visual clutter and improves reader comprehension. My design features a strip of movie tickets, and the text is aligned to the left with relevant information grouped. The website URL for the festival is positioned on top of the tickets, as a visual representation of where to purchase your tickets. This was the design principle I found the hardest to focus on, as it is always such an important principle to adhere to on a poster however, I am pleased with my final result as it is easily comprehended and visually interesting for the reader.

Typographic Systems

Typographic systems are methods of organising text on a page to enusre readability and visual appeal. Below I have researched 8 and created my own designs using Abode Illustrator.

Dilatational

Dilatational typographic systems curve text and elements dynamically around a central point. The circular composition invites the eye to spiral inward, drawing in the viewer. I drew inspiration from Rinko Kawauchi’s M/E collection, particularly the image featuring two children where the sun reflects in a circle through the camera lens. This felt powerful as it invites the viewer to pause and connect, creating emotion. It inspired me to apply a similar technique in my design, allowing the circles of text to pull the reader in. I chose Clarendon as the font because it is easily read, ensuring the text is comprehended with ease despite the potentially distorting typographic system.

Radial

Radial designs use a central focal point, radiating all content out from that centre. Researching this typographic system reminded me of recent flower sketches I had done. I developed these sketches further and used the pen tool in Illustrator to create a vector drawing for my design. I wanted to incorporate nature, a major theme in the exhibit. The words radiating from the flower further push the feelings created with a radial composition. The clean style of the Komet font contrasts with the harsh vector art used in the design.

Random

Random typographic systems arrange elements without a defined pattern, aim, or purpose, creating a playful experience while viewers search for connections. I particularly enjoy designs where text flows in more than one direction, making the eye not want to move away from the design.

Grid

Grid systems organise text and graphics into columns and rows, prioritising readability. I began my design with a 3 x 4 grid to structure my content and positioned the content using rectangles. Futura PT’s clean, modern aesthetic aligned well with the ensure, continuing to prioritise readability.

Axial

Axial designs organize content along an imaginary axis, which can be straight or curved. Content can be symmetrical or asymmetrical, and multiple axes can guide the viewer’s eye to create dynamic compositions. Inspired by an image from Rinko Kawauchi’s Illuminance collection, where sunlight breaks through trees, I explored the natural juxtaposition of soft organic shapes and harsh axis lines. After some sketching, I decided on a dandelion as the centrepiece, which encapsulates the themes of the exhibition while directing the reader’s eye down the page. I used Adobe Garamond Pro for its bold yet clear composition, contrasting with the delicate flower vector.

Modular

Modular layouts use repeating elements, such as shapes, to structure content. My design uses eight squares to contain and separate the text, taking the viewer's eye down to the right side of the design. I used Clarendon font to keep with the clean feel.

Transitional

Transitional compositions aim to create natural & dynamic movement within their compositions, often mirroring the layering of sediment and stone. This was the most challenging composition for me as I struggled to create a sense of things flowing in my design. I used the type-on-a-path tool to give my text movement.

Bilateral

Bilateral systems align content along a single axis, often creating symmetrical designs. For my composition, I used a diagonal axis, aligning all text except the title to the right side. This creates visual interest while maintaining balance and readability.

Reflection

Over my first term studying Graphic and Web Design I have built foundational skills in a range of areas, including Adobe Illustrator and Indesign, website development using HTML and CSS and design and typography principles. For most of these topics, it was the first time I have studied them in an educational environment and have enjoyed being taught and having the space to build these skills.

I have weekly completed coding worksheets taking me through the basics of HTML & CSS. I had limited knowledge in these areas before but now have a good understanding of how to use to HTML structures a website and to CSS provide styling and layout. Using flexbox and creating the carousel reinforced my skills, whilst opening my eyes to the possibilities with just CSS. I felt in a good position to build my website after completing these tasks.

In graphic design, principles are used to ensure visually cohesive and interesting design is achieved. The CARP principles are vital in this. I created the four posters using InDesign, this was the first time I have used this or any vector software. To begin I had many challenges, like how to type along a curved path or change the colour of the text and be able to reuse that colour. This was a valuable learning experience, gradually becoming more proficient with each design.

Researching and implementing the eight typographic systems deepened my understanding of how text can be displayed on a page and how this engages viewers’ attention. I had never used Illustrator before completing this task but with an understanding of InDesign it came quickly, as there are many similarities with the UI. I know there are lots of possibilities with this software I am yet to explore and hope to grow these skills in the future.

The final part of the project was to complete my website to display my work. Beginning by creating a wireframe to map out how it would look and how the different elements would be structured on the page. This made writing the HTML simple. Then styling the web page with CSS, adding colour, defining the layout, and importing Raleway from Google Fonts. I found that when my page was displayed on smaller screens the content did not display properly so I added a media query making the page responsive. To improve the experience for mobile users further, I would use JavaScript to make the navbar collapsable.

Using GitHub to store and host my files is now something I feel confident in and I look forward to furthering these skills by understanding how different branches work.

Through this project I have developed my skills in many areas. I now have a comprehensive understanding of web development, and how to problem solve when issues arrive. I understand how elements relate to each other on a page and which principles to follow to have a desired effect. I look forward to creating more websites, with more complex layouts and user interaction.
